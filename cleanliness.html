Cleanliness

Soldering is a chemical process. Every surface involved — pads, terminations, tip, paste, flux — must be in a known state for the chemistry to work. Contamination is the invisible variable behind most "I did everything right and it still didn't work" failures.

The Core Problem

Solder bonds to clean metal through intermetallic formation. Anything between the solder and the metal — oxides, oils, moisture, old flux, silicone, organic residue — blocks that bond. Flux exists to remove oxides, but flux can't remove everything, and flux itself becomes contamination if left in the wrong state.

Contamination causes failures at every stage:

  • Before soldering: oxidized surfaces won't wet
  • During soldering: trapped moisture and volatiles cause spattering, voiding, and blowouts
  • After soldering: residues cause corrosion, leakage currents, dendritic growth, and long-term field failures

Board Cleanliness

Bare Board Oxidation

Copper oxidizes in air. The rate depends on temperature, humidity, and surface finish — but it's always happening.

The fingerprint problem

A single fingerprint on an OSP or bare copper pad deposits oils, salts, and moisture that break through the protective layer and accelerate oxidation. The print pattern is often visible as selective non-wetting after reflow — solder wets everywhere except the fingerprint outline. Handle boards by the edges. Wear gloves if you must touch the surface.

Board Storage

  • Sealed with desiccant — the standard. Boards ship in vacuum-sealed bags with desiccant and a humidity indicator card. Leave them sealed until ready to use.
  • Once opened: solder within the finish's floor life. For OSP, this is days in humid environments. For ENIG, weeks to months.
  • Long-term storage: reseal with fresh desiccant, or store in a dry cabinet (<10% RH).
  • Don't stack bare boards face-to-face — surface-to-surface contact transfers contamination and abrades finishes.

Board Moisture Absorption

FR4 is hygroscopic. It absorbs moisture from the air over time — typically 0.1-0.3% by weight, depending on thickness and exposure. This moisture becomes a problem during reflow:

  • At >100°C, absorbed water turns to steam
  • Steam expanding inside the laminate causes delamination (measling, blistering)
  • Steam escaping through vias and pads causes solder spattering and voids
  • Boards stored in humid environments for weeks-months should be baked before reflow: typically 125°C for 2-4 hours (follow IPC-1601 for specifics)
For hobby boards

If your PCBs arrived recently and have been in a reasonably dry indoor environment, moisture isn't usually a problem. If they've been sitting in a garage or shed for months, or you're in a tropical climate, give them a bake. A kitchen oven at 125°C for 2 hours works — just don't use it for food afterward if working with leaded materials.

Component Cleanliness

Termination Oxidation

Component leads and terminations oxidize in storage. The rate depends on the plating:

  • Tin-plated (most SMD passives): oxidizes over months. Old stock with dull, gray terminations won't wet easily.
  • Gold-plated: very slow oxidation. But gold dissolves into solder rapidly (gold embrittlement) — a different problem.
  • Solder-dipped: good shelf life, similar to HASL boards.
  • Palladium-silver (some MLCCs): can develop sulfide tarnish. Solder may not wet without aggressive flux.

Test: if solder doesn't wet a component termination with normal flux, the termination is oxidized or contaminated. Try more aggressive flux (RA) before assuming the component is bad.

Moisture Sensitivity

Plastic-packaged ICs absorb moisture through the molding compound. During reflow, this moisture vaporizes:

  • Delamination between die and molding compound
  • Package cracking ("popcorning" — the package visibly swells or pops)
  • Wire bond damage inside the package

This is governed by IPC/JEDEC J-STD-020 Moisture Sensitivity Levels:

The desiccant bag rule

If the IC came in a sealed moisture barrier bag with a desiccant and humidity indicator card: check the card. If the indicator shows <10% RH, the part is fine. If the bag was open or the indicator is pink (>10%), treat it as exceeded floor life and bake before reflow.

For hand soldering with an iron, moisture sensitivity is much less of a concern — the thermal exposure is localized and brief. MSL mostly matters for reflow (full-package heating).

Old / Salvaged Components

Parts pulled from old boards or sitting in bins for years have multiple contamination problems:

  • Oxidized terminations — won't wet without aggressive flux
  • Residual solder with unknown alloy — mixing leaded residue with lead-free creates low-melting-point phases. Bismuth contamination is especially dangerous (ternary eutectic at 96°C).
  • Absorbed moisture — old plastic ICs are guaranteed saturated. Bake before reflow.
  • Unknown provenance — counterfeit components are a real problem in surplus markets. Remarked parts, recycled parts sold as new.

Solder & Paste

Solder Wire

  • Solder wire itself doesn't really go bad — the metal is stable. The flux core can dry out or degrade over years.
  • Store sealed to prevent flux degradation. If wire is many years old and solder doesn't flow smoothly, the flux core may be inactive — add external flux.
  • Oxidized wire surface: wipe with IPA or scrape. Usually cosmetic.

Solder Paste

Paste is far more perishable than wire. It's a suspension of metal powder in flux — and both degrade:

  • Refrigerated shelf life: typically 6 months sealed at 0-10°C
  • Room temperature: flux degrades within weeks. Powder oxidizes. Viscosity changes.
  • After opening: air exposure accelerates oxidation. Use within days to weeks depending on product.
  • On the stencil: 8-12 hours max. Beyond this, flux solvents evaporate, viscosity increases, print quality degrades.
  • Print-to-reflow window: 24 hours max. Flux is activating slowly at room temperature.

Finer powder (Type 4, 5, 6) degrades faster — more surface area means more oxide surface. Type 6 has ~75% more surface area than Type 3.

Condensation kills paste

Paste stored in the fridge must come to room temperature (4-6 hours) before opening. If you open a cold jar, moisture from the air condenses into the paste. Water in solder paste causes explosive spattering during reflow — steam blasts through molten solder, throwing solder balls across the board and creating massive voiding.

Flux Residue

Flux starts as your ally and can become your enemy. The residue left after soldering ranges from harmless to corrosive depending on the flux type.

Dendritic Growth

The worst-case outcome of uncleaned active flux. Ionic contamination + moisture + voltage bias creates metal dendrites that grow between traces until they short:

  • Requires three things: ionic residue (from flux), moisture (from humidity), and an electric field (from the circuit being powered)
  • Grows slowly — may take days, weeks, or months in the field before causing failure
  • More common at fine pitch where trace spacing is small
  • This is why water-soluble flux residue on a powered board in a humid environment is a ticking time bomb

What IPA can't reach

IPA and a brush work for accessible joints. But under QFNs, BGAs, and any component with <0.1mm standoff, capillary action holds flux residue in place. A brush can't reach it. If you used active flux under these components, you need ultrasonic cleaning or a spray wash system. This is one reason no-clean flux dominates modern SMD assembly — the residue can be left in place under low-standoff parts.

Iron Tip

A contaminated or oxidized tip is the number one cause of frustration in hand soldering. The full treatment is in Hand Soldering — Tip Maintenance, but the cleanliness angle:

  • Oxidation — a bare tip at temperature oxidizes within seconds. Oxidized tips can't transfer heat because the oxide layer is thermally insulating. Keep the tip tinned (coated with solder) whenever it's hot.
  • Carbonized flux — flux residue bakes onto the tip over time, forming a dark carbon layer. Brass wool removes it. Wet sponge smears it.
  • Contamination from previous alloys — switching between leaded and lead-free solder on the same tip can deposit intermetallics that degrade wetting. Use separate tips for separate alloys if you work with both.

Work Environment

Airborne Contamination

  • Dust and fibers — settle on boards and pads. Flux can't clean fibers. Causes inclusions in joints and wetting defects.
  • Silicone — the invisible enemy. Silicone-containing products (some sprays, lubricants, release agents, hand creams, certain tapes) contaminate surfaces and completely prevent solder wetting. Silicone spreads easily, is extremely difficult to remove, and the contamination is invisible. Keep silicone products away from the soldering area.
  • Outgassing from materials — foam packaging, certain plastics, and adhesives can outgas compounds that deposit on board surfaces. Don't store boards next to foam or rubber.
  • Sulfur compounds — from cardboard, rubber bands, and industrial environments. Tarnishes silver and immersion tin finishes. Store boards in sealed bags, not cardboard boxes.
The silicone rule

If solder won't wet a surface and flux doesn't help, suspect silicone contamination. Common sources: mold release spray used in nearby manufacturing, silicone-based conformal coating handled before soldering, hand cream, hair products. Silicone contamination requires specialized solvents to remove — IPA won't touch it.

Handling

  • Handle boards by edges only
  • Wear nitrile or lint-free cotton gloves for production work
  • No eating or drinking at the workbench (contamination goes both ways — see Safety & PPE)
  • Don't blow on boards to remove debris — saliva is ionic contamination. Use compressed air (filtered, oil-free) or a soft brush.

Cleanliness and Toxicity

Cleanliness isn't just about solder quality — acidic flux residues make tin more bioavailable. Active flux residue (RA, water-soluble) on a board or on your hands dissolves tin into ionic form (Sn2+), which is readily absorbed through skin and the GI tract. Cleaning flux residue promptly isn't just a reliability practice — it's a health practice.
