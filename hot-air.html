Hot Air Rework

Non-contact heating. Four stages: preheat, soak, reflow, cool. This page covers setup, component removal, placement, and what goes wrong.

When to Use Hot Air

  • SMD component removal — can't get an iron under a QFN
  • SMD component placement — small-batch assembly with solder paste
  • BGA rework — the only practical option for ball grid arrays
  • Multi-pin IC removal — faster and safer than desoldering pin by pin
  • Shrink tubing — low temp (150-200°C), high airflow

Station Setup

Temperature

The station displays air temperature, not joint temperature. Air at 350°C produces joints around 230-250°C.

Verify with a thermocouple

First time with a new station or new component? Tape a thermocouple to the board near the target and verify actual joint temperature. Station readings lie — especially cheap stations.

Nozzle Selection

  • Round nozzles: general purpose. Match diameter to component size. Most common.
  • Square/rectangular: QFP, SOIC — match the IC package outline
  • BGA nozzles: sized to specific BGA footprint
  • No nozzle: wide, unfocused heat. Good for large area preheat. Bad for precision.

Rule: nozzle should be slightly larger than the component but smaller than the gap to neighbors. Too large → heats everything around it. Too small → uneven heating → tombstoning.

Removing a Component

Step 1: Protect Neighbors

  • Shield nearby components with kapton tape or aluminum foil
  • Particularly important for plastic connectors, LEDs, and electrolytic caps — they melt/pop easily
  • If using a stencil-style nozzle, this is less critical

Step 2: Apply Flux

Generously flux all joints on the component. The flux will activate during heating and help the solder melt cleanly.

Step 3: Preheat

  • If you have a bottom-side preheater: set to 100-150°C, wait 1-2 minutes
  • If not: use the hot air at a distance (10-15cm) with wide nozzle for 30-60s to gently warm the area
  • Goal: bring the board to 100-150°C uniformly so the hot air only needs to deliver the last 100°C

Step 4: Heat

  1. Position nozzle 1-2cm above the component
  2. Move in slow, small circles — don't park on one spot
  3. Watch the flux — it will activate (bubble, then clear) as temperature rises
  4. Watch for solder to reflow — on QFP/SOIC you'll see the joints "flash" shiny as they melt
  5. On QFN/BGA: no visible joints. Watch for the component to very slightly shift/settle — this means the solder underneath has melted
Angle matters for passives

For small passives (0402-0805), come in at roughly 45° rather than straight down. Direct vertical airflow pushes down on the component and then deflects sideways off the board surface — creating turbulence that can blow lightweight parts off their pads or shift them during reflow.

At 45°, the air sweeps across the component and both pads more evenly, and the downward force component is reduced. You're heating the joint area rather than pinning and buffeting the part. This is especially important during placement — a 0402 sitting on wet paste has almost no weight holding it in position.

Kyocera's MLCC handling guide states it explicitly: "The angle between the nozzle and the capacitor is recommended to be 45 degrees in order to work easily and to avoid partial area heating." Their full rework conditions also specify nozzle distance ≥5mm, temperature ≤400°C, and blowing duration ≤10s for small sizes (Kyocera MLCC Rework Guide).

Step 5: Remove Component

  1. Once all joints are reflowed, grip with tweezers and lift straight up
  2. Don't slide or twist — you'll drag solder across pads and damage traces
  3. If the component resists: NOT all joints are melted. Keep heating. Don't force it.
  4. Set component aside on a heat-resistant surface
What can go wrong
  • Component won't come free — one or more joints haven't reflowed. Usually a ground pad (thermal mass). More heat, more time, or preheat.
  • Pad lifted with the component — too much force before all solder melted. This is why you never force it.
  • Neighboring component shifted — heat spread too far. Use a smaller nozzle, lower airflow, or shield neighbors.
  • Plastic parts nearby melted — should have shielded them. Kapton tape reflects heat effectively.

Step 6: Clean the Pads

  1. Add flux to the now-empty pads
  2. For QFP/SOIC pads: drag a clean, tinned iron tip across to level the solder
  3. For BGA/QFN pads: use solder wick + flux to remove excess solder, leaving thin, even coating
  4. Clean flux residue with IPA
  5. Inspect under magnification: pads should be flat, evenly tinned, no bridges

Placing a New Component

Solder Paste Method (Best)

  1. Clean pads (IPA + brush)
  2. Apply solder paste to each pad — syringe with fine tip, or stencil for production
  3. Place component with tweezers — align carefully
  4. Heat with hot air: preheat → soak → reflow as described above
  5. Solder paste melts, component self-aligns (surface tension pulls it into position)
  6. Remove heat, let cool

Flux + Existing Solder Method (Quick Rework)

  1. Ensure pads have an even layer of solder from the previous component
  2. Apply generous flux
  3. Place new component on top
  4. Heat with hot air until solder reflows and component settles
  5. This works when there's enough residual solder — if pads are bare, use paste
What can go wrong during placement
  • Component drifts during reflow — too much airflow. Reduce to 30-40%. Or component placed off-center and surface tension is pulling it further off.
  • Tombstoning — one end reflows before the other. More preheat to equalize. Or paste deposit is uneven.
  • Component rotated 90° or 180° — check orientation before heating. Mark pin 1. Once it's reflowed on wrong, you have to remove and redo.
  • Bridges on fine-pitch — too much paste or too much residual solder. Remove component, clean pads with wick, apply fresh paste, try again.

BGA Rework

Ball Grid Arrays have solder joints under the package. You can't see them, can't touch them with an iron, and can't easily verify them.

BGA Removal

  1. Preheat — board to 100-150°C minimum. BGA packages are large thermal masses.
  2. Apply flux around the package edges (it will wick under by capillary action)
  3. Heat with BGA nozzle — matched to package size. 340-380°C air.
  4. Monitor with thermocouple if possible — peak should hit 235-245°C at the joint
  5. Watch for the settle — when all balls melt, the package will drop very slightly (~0.1mm)
  6. Lift straight up with vacuum pickup tool or tweezers

BGA Placement

  1. Clean pads with wick and flux — must be perfectly flat
  2. Apply flux or paste to pads
  3. If the new BGA has pre-attached balls: flux and place
  4. If reballing: use stencil + paste or pre-formed balls (solder sphere kit)
  5. Align with a BGA alignment fixture or under magnification
  6. Reflow with profile above
Inspecting BGA joints

You can't see BGA joints. Options:

  • X-ray inspection (production — you probably don't have this)
  • Continuity/resistance testing — check key signal and power pins
  • Gentle push test — a properly reflowed BGA doesn't shift
  • Thermal imaging — look for hot spots indicating open connections

Don't have a multi-zone station?

Most hobby hot air stations don't have programmable profiles. Approximate the zones manually: start with the gun further away (preheat), move closer over 30-60 seconds (soak), then hold at working distance until reflow. The key principle is the same — gradual temperature rise, not hitting the component with max heat immediately.
