Preheat

Bringing the board up to temperature before soldering. The difference between consistent joints, cracked ceramics, and lifted pads.

Why Preheat

  1. Prevents thermal shock — a ceramic 0402 capacitor going from 25°C to 250°C in 2 seconds can crack internally. You won't see it. It'll fail in the field.
  2. Activates flux early — solvents evaporate, flux starts cleaning the surfaces before the iron/air arrives
  3. Equalizes temperature — a 4-layer board has massive copper planes that create cold spots. Preheat eliminates them.
  4. Reduces iron workload — if the board is already at 120°C, your iron only needs to deliver the last 130°C to reach reflow. Without preheat, it has to deliver all 230°C through a tiny tip.
  5. Reduces dwell time — less time with iron on the pad = less risk of pad lift, less flux charring

Maximum preheat temperature

Don't exceed Tg - 10°C where Tg is the board's glass transition temperature. Standard FR4 Tg is 130-180°C depending on grade. Above Tg, the board softens and can deform — especially with components on the bottom side.

Ramp Rates

How fast you bring the board up to temperature matters as much as the temperature itself.

ParameterValueWhy
Recommended ramp1.5-3°C/secSafe for all components including ceramics
Maximum ramp4-5°C/secAbove this, thermal shock risk increases sharply
Stabilization time60-90 secondsHold at target temp to let the whole board equalize

A board that reads 120°C on the surface may still be 60°C in the center of a ground plane. The stabilization time lets heat conduct inward.

When to Preheat

Always Preheat For

  • Hot air rework — any hot air work benefits from preheat
  • BGA / QFN removal or placement
  • Multilayer boards (4+ layers)
  • Pads connected to large copper pours or ground planes
  • Lead-free soldering on boards with significant thermal mass
  • Components rated MSL (moisture sensitivity level) > 1 — moisture inside the package can cause "popcorning" during rapid heating

Optional But Recommended

  • General through-hole on 2-layer boards — helps with ground-connected pins
  • Small SMD rework on simple boards — reduces the temperature your iron needs to reach
  • Any time you're struggling with heat delivery and a bigger tip isn't an option

How to Preheat

Board-Level Preheater (Best)

Dedicated IR or convection preheater. Sets under the PCB holder.

  1. Set to 100-150°C (start low, verify with thermocouple)
  2. Place board on preheater
  3. Wait 1-2 minutes for temperature to stabilize
  4. Verify board surface temp with IR thermometer or thermocouple
  5. Now solder/rework as normal — you'll notice the difference immediately

Cost: $50-200 for a hobby-grade IR preheater. Worth it for lead-free work.

Hot Plate

A laboratory hot plate or even a kitchen electric griddle works.

  • Set to 100-150°C
  • Place board directly on the surface (or on a thin aluminum plate for even heat)
  • Wait for stabilization
  • Watch for components on the bottom side — they'll be in direct contact with the heat source

Hot Air Nozzle Preheat

No dedicated preheater? Use your hot air station.

  1. Use a wide nozzle, low airflow (30-40%)
  2. Set to 150-180°C
  3. Circle the target area from 10-15cm away for 30-60 seconds
  4. Then switch to your normal rework nozzle and proceed

This is a compromise — the heat is less uniform than a bottom preheater.

No Preheater At All

The compensation game

Without a preheater, your iron has to deliver all the energy. Compensate with:

  • Biggest possible tip
  • Higher temperature (380-420°C)
  • More flux (to improve heat transfer)
  • Accept longer dwell times (but not more than 5 seconds)
  • This works for simple boards but will frustrate you on multilayer boards with ground planes.
