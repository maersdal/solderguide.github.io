SMD Prototyping

Building a surface-mount board from bare PCB to working prototype. The complete workflow with every method — from syringe-and-hot-air to stencil-and-reflow.

The Workflow

  1. Get your PCB and stencil (or don't — syringe works too)
  2. Apply solder paste to the pads
  3. Place components on the paste
  4. Reflow — melt the paste with heat
  5. Inspect and fix — bridges, cold joints, missing parts
  6. Hand-solder anything that can't be reflowed (through-hole, connectors, large parts)

The whole process for a simple board takes 30-60 minutes once you've done it a few times.

Applying Solder Paste

Method 1: Stencil (Best Results)

A laser-cut stainless steel or kapton stencil with apertures matching your pads. This is how production does it, and it's accessible for hobby use.

  1. Order your stencil with your PCB. Most PCB fabs (JLCPCB, PCBWay, OSHPark) offer stencils for $5-15. Order frameless — you'll tape it down yourself.
  2. Align the stencil. Tape the PCB to your work surface. Place the stencil on top, align apertures to pads. Tape the stencil down on one edge as a hinge.
  3. Apply paste. Squeeze a bead of paste along one edge of the stencil. Use a squeegee (old credit card works) to drag the paste across the apertures at a 45° angle. One smooth pass.
  4. Lift the stencil. Straight up, don't slide. You should see clean paste deposits on every pad.
  5. Inspect. Check for bridged deposits (too much paste), missing deposits (blocked aperture), or smeared deposits (stencil shifted). Fix with a toothpick or redo.
What can go wrong with stencils
  • Paste smears between pads — stencil wasn't flat against the board. Use more tape. Or the paste is too warm (should be room temp, brought from fridge 4-6 hours before use).
  • Paste doesn't release from apertures — paste too cold or too old. Stencil too thick for the aperture size (area ratio problem). Clean the underside and try again.
  • Stencil doesn't align — the stencil and PCB are from different fabs, or one has slightly different dimensions. Use alignment pins or fiducials if available. For hobby: eyeball it under magnification.

Method 2: Syringe (No Stencil)

For one-offs and prototypes where ordering a stencil isn't worth it.

  1. Get SAC305 paste in a syringe with a fine dispensing tip (20-22 gauge)
  2. Apply a small dot to each pad — just enough to cover it
  3. For fine-pitch ICs: a thin line across all pads works. Surface tension separates during reflow.
  4. For QFN thermal pads: fill about 50-70% of the pad area. Too much = voiding. Too little = poor thermal connection.
Syringe technique

Hold at 45°, squeeze gently, lift cleanly. The dot should be about the size of the pad — no bigger. If it's a blob that covers neighboring pads, you used too much. Wipe off with IPA and redo. Practice on a scrap board first.

Method 3: Hand-Transfer (Desperation Mode)

No paste at all? You can tin each pad with an iron, apply flux, place the component, and reflow with hot air. It works. It's slow. But it works.

Placing Components

Placement Order

  1. Smallest components first — 0402/0603 passives. They're hardest to place and easiest to disturb.
  2. ICs next — QFP, SOIC, QFN. Align pin 1 carefully.
  3. Larger passives and connectors last — they won't move when you bump the board.

Orientation Matters

  • Polarized capacitors — check the stripe (negative) or marking
  • Diodes/LEDs — cathode mark matches pad marking on silkscreen
  • ICs — pin 1 dot on the package matches pin 1 indicator on the board. Double check before reflow. Rotating an IC after reflow means full rework.
What can go wrong during placement
  • Component slides off the paste — paste may have dried (stencil life exceeded) or the dot was too small. Add fresh paste to that pad.
  • Component sticks to tweezers instead of paste — clean the tweezer tips. Or use a vacuum pen.
  • Can't tell which way the IC goes — look for the pin 1 indicator: a dot, notch, or bevel on the package. Cross-reference with the datasheet. If you can't tell, don't guess.

Tweezers and Small Parts

Tweezers are the main placement tool, but they're also the main way to destroy small components. A 0402 or 0201 capacitor cracks easily under squeeze pressure — especially MLCCs, which are brittle ceramic. Pick up from the ends, not the flat faces, and use the minimum grip force needed. If a part keeps shooting away when you try to grab it, your tweezers are dull or misaligned — replace or re-sharpen them rather than squeezing harder.

Soldering Small Passives by Hand

Hot air isn't the only option. For one or two small passives, hand soldering with drag technique is fast:

  1. Tin one pad with the iron
  2. Hold the component with tweezers, reflow the tinned pad to tack it in place
  3. Release the tweezers — the component is now held by one joint
  4. Solder the other end normally
  5. Go back and reflow the first joint with a little extra solder if needed

This avoids the airflow problems of hot air entirely. For 0201s, do this under magnification.

When to use hot air vs iron for small passives

Hot air excels when you're reflowing many components at once — paste the whole board and reflow an area. But for placing or reworking individual small passives, a fine-tip iron with the tack-and-solder technique above is faster and avoids the risk of blowing parts around or disturbing neighbors.

Reflow Methods

Method 1: Hot Air Station

Best for single boards, rework, and boards with a mix of SMD and through-hole.

  1. Preheat the board — bottom preheater to 100-150°C if you have one
  2. Set hot air to 300-340°C, airflow 40-50%
  3. Start from one corner, move slowly across the board
  4. Watch the paste — it goes from grey paste → flux activation (bubbling) → shiny liquid (reflowed)
  5. Once all joints have reflowed, remove heat
  6. Let cool naturally — don't blow on it
Hot air pitfalls
  • Small parts blow away — reduce airflow to 30%. Or use a nozzle focused on larger components and let radiant heat handle the small ones.
  • Uneven reflow — one side of the board reflowed, other side still paste. Move the nozzle more slowly. Or preheat.
  • Tombstoning on small passives — uneven heating. Preheat solves this.

Method 2: Hot Plate

Cheap, effective, great for single-sided boards. A laboratory hot plate or a cheap electric griddle.

  1. Place board on the cold plate
  2. Set temperature to 250°C (or use a ramp profile if your plate supports it)
  3. Watch the paste — same visual cues as hot air
  4. When all joints reflow, remove the board with pliers or spatula
  5. Let cool on a heat-resistant surface
ProsCons
Dead simpleNo top-side heat — components with thermal pads may not fully reflow
Even heat across the boardOnly works for single-sided boards (bottom side touches the plate)
$30-60 for a usable plateTemperature overshoot if the plate has poor control
No risk of blowing componentsSlow ramp — can burn flux if the plate takes too long to reach peak
Hot plate + hot air combo

The best hobby setup: hot plate at 150-180°C as a preheater, then hot air from the top to push through to reflow. This gives you the even base heating of a plate plus the targeted top-side heating of hot air. Works for double-sided boards too.

Method 3: Toaster/Reflow Oven

A modified toaster oven with a controller (like a Controleo3 or DIY PID controller) that follows a real reflow profile.

  1. Place board in the oven
  2. Run the profile — the controller manages preheat, soak, reflow, and cooling
  3. Remove board after cooling
ProsCons
Real reflow profile — best resultsNeeds a modified oven + controller ($80-200 total)
Hands-off — repeatableSetup time for the first build
Handles double-sided boardsNot great for rework (whole board reflows)
Even heatingCan't selectively heat one area

Method 4: Skillet / Frying Pan

Yes, really. A cast-iron skillet on a stove works in a pinch. Same principle as a hot plate but less control. Monitor with a thermocouple. Do not use the skillet for food afterward.

Method 5: Vapor Phase Reflow

The board sits in the vapor of a boiling fluid (e.g. Galden or Solvay perfluoropolyether). The fluid has a specific boiling point — typically 230°C or 240°C for lead-free — and the vapor condenses on the board, transferring heat extremely efficiently. When every surface reaches the fluid's boiling point, condensation stops and the temperature cannot overshoot. That's the key advantage.

  1. Pour the heat transfer fluid into the vapor phase chamber
  2. Place board on the carrier/basket
  3. Lower the board into the vapor zone (or the machine does this automatically)
  4. Vapor condenses on the board, heating it uniformly
  5. Once the board reaches the fluid's boiling point, reflow is complete
  6. Raise the board out of the vapor zone to cool
ProsCons
Physically impossible to overshoot — peak temp is locked to the fluid's boiling pointThe heat transfer fluid is expensive ($200-500/liter for Galden)
Perfectly uniform heating — vapor condenses on every surface equallyDedicated equipment needed (hobby-level machines exist but aren't cheap)
Inert atmosphere — no oxidation during reflowFluid must be recovered and reused carefully
Excellent for lead-free — the tight peak control is exactly what SAC305 needsLess common, so less community knowledge and troubleshooting help
Why vapor phase gives perfect temperature control

The physics are elegant: the vapor can only exist at the fluid's boiling point. When it contacts a cooler surface (your board), it condenses and releases its latent heat of vaporization — a large amount of energy delivered uniformly. Once the board surface reaches the boiling point, no more condensation occurs, so no more heat is transferred. You literally cannot overheat the board beyond the fluid's boiling point, no matter how long you leave it in the vapor.

Working with QFN / DFN Packages

QFN (Quad Flat No-lead) packages have pads underneath the package with no visible leads. They're everywhere in modern designs and they terrify people. Here's how to handle them.

The Challenge

  • Pads are under the chip — you can't see or touch them with an iron
  • Most QFNs have a large thermal/ground pad in the center — this is a massive heat sink
  • The thermal pad needs solder for both electrical connection and heat dissipation
  • Too much paste on the thermal pad = voiding = poor thermal performance

QFN Paste Application

  • Signal pads: normal paste deposits, same as any other pad
  • Thermal pad: apply paste to about 50-70% of the pad area. A grid pattern or cross pattern works well — leave gaps for volatiles to escape during reflow.
  • If using a stencil: the stencil should have a windowed aperture on the thermal pad (grid of smaller openings rather than one big one)

QFN Reflow

  • Preheat is critical — the thermal pad is a heat sink
  • Hot air: 320-360°C, positioned directly above the QFN. Watch for the chip to settle slightly when solder underneath melts.
  • Hot plate: works well because heat comes from below, directly through the board into the thermal pad
  • Verify: gently push the chip sideways — if it moves, it's not reflowed. If it resists and springs back slightly, it's soldered.
QFN gotchas
  • Voiding under thermal pad — too much paste, insufficient soak time, or paste not degassed. Reduce paste volume, extend soak.
  • Open connections on signal pads — thermal pad sucked all the solder via capillary action. Use more paste on signal pads, or reduce thermal pad paste.
  • Can't tell if it's soldered — check continuity on the thermal pad (usually ground). Check signal pads with a multimeter.

Fine-Pitch Components (0.5mm and Below)

QFP, TSSOP, and other packages with 0.5mm pin pitch or less.

With Paste + Reflow

  • Stencil is strongly recommended at 0.5mm pitch — syringe deposits are hard to control at this scale
  • Use Type 4 paste (20-38 μm powder) for 0.5mm pitch — Type 3 may not print cleanly in the small apertures
  • Align the IC perfectly before reflow — surface tension will correct minor misalignment but won't fix a rotated IC
  • Inspect after reflow under magnification — bridges are common and expected at this pitch

With Iron (Drag Soldering)

See Hand Soldering — Drag Soldering section. This works well for prototyping when you don't have paste.

Double-Sided Boards

Components on both sides of the PCB. The second side reflow is the scary part — will the first side fall off?

Why Components Don't Fall Off

Surface tension holds them. When solder melts on the bottom side during second reflow, the surface tension of the molten solder acts as glue. For components up to about 0.5g, this is reliable. Heavier components (large ICs, connectors) may need mechanical retention or should go on the second-reflow side.

Strategy

  1. First side: populate the side with fewer and smaller components. Reflow.
  2. Flip the board. The first-side components are now hanging upside down.
  3. Second side: apply paste, place components, reflow. The first-side joints will remelt briefly but surface tension keeps them in place.
  4. Heavy components (connectors, transformers, large electrolytics): hand-solder after both reflow cycles.
What can go wrong
  • Heavy component falls off during second reflow — too heavy for surface tension. Glue it with a dot of adhesive before reflow, or hand-solder it afterward.
  • First-side components shift during second reflow — board was bumped or airflow was too high. Handle gently. Reduce airflow if using hot air.

Post-Reflow Inspection

What to Check

  • Bridges — solder connecting adjacent pins. Extremely common on fine-pitch. Fix: flux + drag iron tip, or flux + solder wick.
  • Insufficient solder — pad visible through thin solder, or no fillet. Fix: add paste or solder wire, reflow.
  • Tombstoned passives — one end up. Remove, clean pads, re-paste, re-place, reflow.
  • Shifted/rotated ICs — check pin 1 alignment. If wrong: remove with hot air, clean pads, redo.
  • Missing components — fell off during handling or blown off by hot air. Replace.
