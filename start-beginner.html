Getting Started

You've never soldered, or you've made some joints but aren't confident. This path will get you making solid, reliable connections in an afternoon.

What Is Soldering?

Soldering is joining two metal surfaces with a molten filler metal (solder) that melts at a lower temperature than the parts being joined. The solder melts, flows onto the metal surfaces, and when it cools, it forms a permanent electrical and mechanical connection.

In electronics, that means connecting component leads and terminations to copper pads on a circuit board (PCB). Every resistor, capacitor, and chip on a circuit board is soldered in place — sometimes hundreds or thousands of joints per board.

The Three Ingredients

  1. Heat — from a soldering iron tip (300-400°C) or hot air. Melts the solder and activates the flux. Heat the joint, not the solder — solder flows toward the hottest part of the joint.
  2. Solder — a metal alloy (usually mostly tin) that melts at 217-227°C for lead-free. Comes as wire with flux inside, or as paste for surface-mount work. Forms the actual metallic bond.
  3. Flux — a chemical that cleans the metal surfaces during soldering. Copper oxidizes instantly at soldering temperatures — without flux, solder will not bond to the copper. The flux in your solder wire is a start, but you'll almost always need additional external flux from a pen or syringe.

What Are You Actually Doing?

You're making a metallurgical bond — not just sticking things together. When molten solder touches clean copper, tin atoms from the solder diffuse into the copper and form a new compound (an intermetallic) at the interface. This is a chemical bond at the atomic level. That's why a soldered joint is both electrically conductive and mechanically strong.

Where Soldering Is Used

  • Electronics assembly — connecting components to circuit boards. From phones to satellites.
  • Repair and rework — replacing failed components, fixing broken connections.
  • Prototyping — building one-off circuits by hand before committing to production.
  • Wire connections — joining wires to terminals, connectors, and other wires.
  • Hobbyist projects — building kits, custom electronics, art installations, robots.

Two Types of Components

Every technique on this site falls into one of two categories:

  • Through-hole — components with wire leads that poke through holes in the board. You solder from the back side. This is where everyone starts — bigger parts, easier to see, more forgiving.
  • Surface mount (SMD) — components that sit flat on the board surface. No holes. Smaller parts, tighter spacing. Most modern electronics are surface mount. You'll get here once you're comfortable with through-hole.

The 30-minute test

Read Heat Science and Hand Soldering, then solder 20 through-hole joints on a practice board. If they all have smooth concave fillets with < 90° wetting angle, you're ready for real projects.
